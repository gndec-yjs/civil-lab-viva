# 1. CEMENT TESTS

---

## 1.1 Fineness Test

**Principle:** Finer cement particles present a greater surface area for hydration, accelerating early strength gain. Measured by Blaine's air permeability method or by sieving on 90 µm IS sieve.

**Apparatus:** Blaine's air permeability apparatus, 90 µm IS sieve, balance, stopwatch

**Procedure Keywords:** Prepare cement bed in cell → pass air through → measure time → compute specific surface area (Blaine method); or sieve 100 g cement on 90 µm sieve, weigh residue (sieve method)

**IS Code:** `IS 4031 (Part 1) : 1996` — Sieve Method | `IS 4031 (Part 2) : 1999` — Blaine Method *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Typical Values:**

| Cement Type | Blaine Fineness (m²/kg) |
|---|---|
| OPC 33 Grade | ≥ 225 |
| OPC 43 Grade | ≥ 225 |
| OPC 53 Grade | ≥ 225 |
| PPC (IS 1489) | ≥ 300 |
| PSC / GGBS blend | ≥ 400 |
| Residue on 90 µm sieve | ≤ 10% |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. Define fineness of cement.**
> The specific surface area of cement particles, expressed in m²/kg (Blaine) or as % residue on 90 µm IS sieve.

**Q2. Which method is more accurate — Blaine or sieve method?**
> Blaine's method is more accurate as it measures the total specific surface area. Sieve method only identifies coarser particles and misses fine distribution.

**Q3. What is the residue limit on 90 µm sieve for OPC?**
> Not more than 10% by weight (IS 269:2015 for OPC).

**Q4. What does Blaine fineness of 320 m²/kg indicate?**
> For every kilogram of cement, the total particle surface area is 320 m². Higher value = finer cement = faster hydration.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q5. Why is very fine cement not always desirable on construction sites?**
> - High heat of hydration → thermal cracking in mass concrete
> - Increased water demand → higher w/c ratio if not controlled
> - Faster setting → reduced workability window
> - Greater shrinkage on drying

**Q6. How does fineness affect durability of concrete?**
> Finer cement fills pores more effectively → denser microstructure → lower permeability → better resistance to chloride, sulphate, and carbonation attack.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q7. Two cements have same 28-day strength but different Blaine values. Which would you prefer for a hot-weather concreting job in Punjab?**
> The coarser cement (lower Blaine value). It generates less heat of hydration, reducing thermal gradient and risk of early cracking — critical in summer concreting above 35°C.

**Q8. Can fineness be increased in the field? How?**
> No — fineness is set during cement manufacturing (grinding stage). It cannot be altered on site. This is why cement quality must be verified on delivery via manufacturer's test certificate.

## 1.2 Standard Consistency Test

**Principle:** Determines the water content (as % of cement weight) required to produce a standard paste that allows the Vicat plunger to penetrate to a depth of 33–35 mm from the bottom of the mould (i.e., 5–7 mm from top). This reference water content is used for all subsequent cement tests.

**Apparatus:** Vicat apparatus with 10 mm dia. plunger, mould (80 mm dia., 40 mm deep), balance, measuring cylinder, trowel

**Procedure Keywords:** Mix cement + trial water (26–33%) → fill Vicat mould → level surface → release plunger → record penetration → repeat with adjusted water until 33–35 mm penetration achieved

**IS Code:** `IS 4031 (Part 4) : 1988` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Typical Values:**

| Cement Type | Standard Consistency (P) |
|---|---|
| OPC (General) | 26% – 33% |
| PPC | 28% – 35% |
| PSC | 30% – 38% |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. What is standard consistency of cement?**
> The percentage of water by weight of cement that produces a paste in which the Vicat plunger settles at 33–35 mm from the bottom of the mould.

**Q2. Why is the plunger penetration criterion set at 33–35 mm (not 0 mm or full depth)?**
> At 33–35 mm, the paste is neither too stiff nor too fluid — it represents the standard reference state for initiating hydration tests reproducibly.

**Q3. What is the standard plunger diameter in the consistency test?**
> 10 mm diameter (as per IS 4031 Part 4).

**Q4. What does 'P' represent in cement testing?**
> P = Standard Consistency water percentage. Used in formulas for setting time (0.85P for initial setting time test).

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q5. Why must all cement tests use water at standard consistency (or a defined multiple of P)?**
> Water content directly affects the rate of hydration. If water varies between labs, test results (setting time, strength) become non-comparable. Standardizing water content ensures reproducibility.

**Q6. If a cement shows P = 35% (unusually high), what does it indicate?**
> High consistency water demand may indicate: (a) very fine cement (high surface area), (b) presence of clay or inert filler contamination, or (c) high C₃A content. Such cement has higher water demand in concrete mixes.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q7. For setting time test, why is water added as 0.85P instead of P?**
> In the setting time test, the Vicat needle (1 mm dia.) is more sensitive than the plunger. Adding 0.85P produces a slightly stiffer paste that gives a clearer transition point between initial and final set.

**Q8. In summer, a site engineer notices that cement consistency increases. Is this expected?**
> Yes. Higher ambient temperature accelerates hydration — the paste stiffens faster. Also, if cement has absorbed moisture from humid storage, P increases. Both conditions demand freshness checks on delivery.

---

## 1.3 Setting Time Test

**Principle:** Cement paste progressively loses plasticity and gains rigidity due to the hydration of C₃S and C₃A compounds. Setting time defines the minimum working window (initial set) and the time to achieve structural rigidity (final set).

**Apparatus:** Vicat apparatus with 1 mm sq. needle (initial set) and annular needle (final set), mould (80 mm dia., 40 mm deep), water bath at 27°C ± 2°C

**Procedure Keywords:** Prepare paste at 0.85P water → fill mould → note time of mixing (T₀) → release 1 mm needle every 2 min → initial set = needle penetration of 33–35 mm from bottom → apply annular needle → final set = no visible impression on surface

**IS Code:** `IS 4031 (Part 5) : 1988` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Specification (IS 269:2015 for OPC 43/53 Grade):**

| Setting Time | Limit |
|---|---|
| Initial Setting Time | ≥ 30 minutes |
| Final Setting Time | ≤ 600 minutes (10 hours) |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. Define initial and final setting time of cement.**
> **Initial:** Time from water addition to when cement paste loses plasticity and resists the 1 mm needle (penetration = 33–35 mm from bottom).
> **Final:** Time when cement paste gains full rigidity — annular needle leaves no impression on the surface.

**Q2. Why is the setting time test done at 27°C ± 2°C?**
> Hydration rate is temperature-sensitive. Standardizing at 27°C simulates typical Indian field conditions and ensures comparable results across labs.

**Q3. What is the difference between setting and hardening of cement?**
> **Setting:** Loss of plasticity (no significant strength gain).
> **Hardening:** Post-setting progressive strength development (days to months).

**Q4. Which compound primarily controls initial and final setting?**
> C₃A controls early setting (initial); C₃S governs strength development and influences final setting.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q5. Why is gypsum added to cement during manufacture?**
> Gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) retards the rapid hydration of C₃A — preventing flash set (premature stiffening). It reacts with C₃A to form ettringite, which coats C₃A particles and slows further reaction.

**Q6. What is flash set vs. false set?**
> - **Flash set (true set):** Rapid irreversible stiffening due to excessive C₃A hydration (insufficient gypsum). Cannot be remixed.
> - **False set (stiffening):** Premature stiffening due to dehydration of gypsum (forms hemihydrate during hot grinding). Reversible by continued mixing without adding water.

**Q7. Why does cement set faster in hot weather? What is the site implication?**
> Higher temperature accelerates C₃A and C₃S hydration rates. On site: concrete must be placed faster, mixing time reduced, retarders may be needed, and scheduling shifted to cooler hours (early morning / night concreting).

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q8. A cement sample shows initial setting time of 20 minutes. Is it acceptable? What are the site implications?**
> No — minimum IST as per IS 269:2015 is 30 minutes. This cement has failed the test. On site, this means insufficient time for mixing, transporting, and placing → concrete may not be properly consolidated. Reject the cement lot and retest with fresh sample.

**Q9. Why does adding excess water to concrete delay setting but reduce strength?**
> Excess water dilutes cement paste → reduces concentration of hydrating ions → slower reaction rate → delayed set. Simultaneously, excess water creates capillary pores on evaporation → increased porosity → lower compressive strength. This is the classic workability vs. strength trade-off.

**Q10. Can you determine the quality of cement by setting time alone?**
> No. Setting time only confirms workability window and gypsum adequacy. Strength, soundness, fineness, and chemical composition must all be verified. A cement can have acceptable setting time but fail in soundness or strength.

## 1.4 Soundness Test (Le-Chatelier Method)

**Principle:** Detects the presence of excessive free lime (CaO) and magnesia (MgO) in cement. These expand on delayed hydration after concrete has hardened, causing disruptive volumetric expansion, cracking, and disintegration — called unsoundness.

**Apparatus:** Le-Chatelier mould (split cylinder, 30 mm dia., 30 mm height), glass plates, water bath at 27°C ± 2°C, boiling water bath

**Procedure Keywords:** Fill Le-Chatelier mould with paste (at P% water) → cover with glass plates → submerge in water at 27°C for 24 hr → measure indicator distance (d₁) → boil in water for 3 hrs → cool → measure distance (d₂) → Expansion = d₂ − d₁

**IS Code:** `IS 4031 (Part 3) : 1988` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Specification:**

| Parameter | Limit (IS 269:2015) |
|---|---|
| Le-Chatelier Expansion | ≤ 10 mm |
| Autoclave Expansion (for magnesia) | ≤ 0.8% |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. What does soundness of cement indicate?**
> The ability of cement paste to maintain constant volume after setting and hardening — absence of destructive delayed expansion.

**Q2. Which compounds cause unsoundness?**
> - **Free CaO (free lime):** Hydrates slowly → expansive → causes cracking
> - **Free MgO (periclase):** Hydrates even more slowly (months/years) → delayed unsoundness
> - **Excess SO₃:** Leads to ettringite formation after setting → expansive

**Q3. Why is boiling done in the Le-Chatelier test?**
> To accelerate the hydration of free lime under elevated temperature — simulating years of field exposure in a few hours, making the test practical.

**Q4. What does the Le-Chatelier mould measure?**
> The indicator arms of the split mould spread apart due to expansion of the cement paste inside. The spread distance (d₂ − d₁) in mm = Le-Chatelier expansion.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q5. Why does free lime cause more damage than combined lime (CaO in C₃S)?**
> Combined lime is part of calcium silicate crystal structure and does not expand. Free lime (uncombined CaO) reacts with water to form Ca(OH)₂ — a volume-increasing reaction that occurs after concrete has hardened, generating internal pressure and cracking.

**Q6. Why is the autoclave test used to detect MgO unsoundness?**
> MgO hydrates extremely slowly at normal temperature — Le-Chatelier boiling test is insufficient. The autoclave (high-pressure steam at 216°C) forces rapid hydration of MgO periclase, revealing its expansive potential in an accelerated manner.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q7. A concrete structure develops delayed map cracking 2–3 years after construction. The cement was tested and found sound. What could explain this?**
> Possible causes: (a) cement test was done on freshly manufactured batch but site used older stock with more free MgO; (b) excessive sulphate attack from soil/groundwater causing delayed ettringite formation (DEF); (c) alkali-silica reaction (ASR) if reactive aggregates were used. Soundness test at the time of delivery does not guarantee long-term performance under aggressive exposure.

---

## 1.5 Compressive Strength of Cement (Mortar Cube Test)

**Principle:** Measures the load-bearing capacity of a standard cement-sand mortar cube, confirming that cement meets minimum strength requirements for structural use.

**Apparatus:** Cube moulds (70.6 mm × 70.6 mm × 70.6 mm), compression testing machine (CTM), tamping rod, vibrating machine

**Procedure Keywords:** Mix cement : standard sand = 1:3 (by weight) at w/c = 0.4 → fill moulds in 2 layers → compact → cure in water at 27°C → test at 3 days, 7 days, and 28 days → report average of 3 cubes

**IS Code:** `IS 4031 (Part 6) : 1988` *(Reaffirmed 2021)* | Standard Sand: `IS 650 : 1991`

**Minimum Strength (IS 269:2015):**

| Cement Grade | 3-Day (MPa) | 7-Day (MPa) | 28-Day (MPa) |
|---|---|---|---|
| OPC 33 Grade | 16 | 22 | 33 |
| OPC 43 Grade | 23 | 33 | 43 |
| OPC 53 Grade | 27 | 37 | 53 |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. Why is standard sand used instead of normal sand in the mortar cube test?**
> Standard sand (IS 650) has a defined grading, specific gravity, and is free from impurities. Using it ensures results reflect only the cement quality — not variability in sand properties.

**Q2. What is the w/c ratio used in mortar cube test?**
> 0.4 (fixed, as per IS 4031 Part 6). This does not vary with consistency — unlike other cement tests.

**Q3. Why is the cube size 70.6 mm (not 100 mm or 150 mm)?**
> 70.6 mm corresponds to a face area of approximately 50 cm², allowing the CTM load reading to be directly divided by 50 to get strength in MPa — a calculation convenience built into the standard.

**Q4. How many cubes are tested per batch, and what is the acceptance criterion?**
> 3 cubes. Average of 3 is taken. No individual cube result should fall below the minimum by more than 10%.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q5. Why does OPC 53 Grade gain strength faster than OPC 33 Grade?**
> OPC 53 is finer and has higher C₃S content → faster early hydration → higher early and 28-day strength. OPC 33 has lower fineness and C₃S content — suitable for non-structural or mass concrete work.

**Q6. If 7-day mortar cube strength is adequate but 28-day strength fails, what are the probable causes?**
> (a) Cement stored in humid conditions — partial pre-hydration reduces later strength gain; (b) incorrect sand-cement ratio during casting; (c) improper curing (drying out before 28 days); (d) batch-to-batch variability in cement clinker quality.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q7. A contractor uses OPC 43 Grade to target M30 concrete. Is this appropriate?**
> Yes, OPC 43 Grade is suitable for M30 concrete. The cement grade indicates mortar strength under standard conditions; actual concrete strength depends on mix design (w/c ratio, aggregate quality, compaction). With proper mix design, OPC 43 can achieve M35–M40 concrete.

**Q8. The mortar cube achieves 45 MPa at 28 days, but the concrete cube from the same cement achieves only 28 MPa. Why?**
> Mortar cube uses standard sand and w/c = 0.4 under controlled lab conditions. Concrete cube uses actual aggregates (variable quality), higher w/c ratio (for workability), site compaction (variable), and field curing (less controlled). Each factor reduces in-situ concrete strength relative to mortar strength.

# 2. AGGREGATE TESTS

---

## 2.1 Aggregate Crushing Value (ACV)

**Principle:** Measures the resistance of aggregate to gradual compressive loading. A lower ACV indicates stronger, more resistant aggregate.

**Apparatus:** Steel cylinder (150 mm dia.), plunger, CTM, sieves (12.5 mm, 10 mm, 2.36 mm), balance

**Procedure Keywords:** Aggregate (passing 12.5 mm, retained 10 mm) → fill cylinder in 3 layers → apply 400 kN over 10 minutes → sieve on 2.36 mm → ACV (%) = (fines/total) × 100

**IS Code:** `IS 2386 (Part IV) : 1963` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Typical Values:**

| Application | Maximum ACV |
|---|---|
| Wearing Course (Concrete) | ≤ 30% |
| Base Course (WBM/WMM) | ≤ 40% |
| Sub-base | ≤ 50% |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. Why is the load applied slowly (over 10 minutes) in ACV test?**
> To simulate gradual static compressive loading. Sudden application would introduce dynamic effects, converting the test from ACV to an impact-type test with different results.

**Q2. Why is 2.36 mm sieve used for ACV?**
> Fines below 2.36 mm are considered "crushed" — they have lost their structural size. This sieve size is standardized for comparability across labs.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q3. Why is ACV important in concrete design, not just pavement design?**
> In concrete, aggregates carry compressive load by direct contact (aggregate interlock). Weak aggregates (high ACV) crush under load → reduce concrete stiffness and strength → aggregate becomes the weak link, not the paste.

**Q4. What is the 10% Fines Value (TFV) and why is it more precise than ACV?**
> TFV measures the load required to produce exactly 10% fines — more sensitive for strong aggregates where ACV would give very low (<10%) values and lose resolution. TFV is preferred for high-quality aggregates used in structural concrete.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q5. An aggregate has ACV = 18%. A contractor claims this is excellent. Is he correct?**
> Yes — ACV of 18% is excellent (well below 30% limit for wearing course). However, ACV alone is insufficient. AIV, LA Abrasion, FI, EI, specific gravity, and water absorption must all be verified before accepting the aggregate.

---

## 2.2 Aggregate Impact Value (AIV)

**Principle:** Measures resistance of aggregate to sudden dynamic/impact loading — simulating vehicle wheel loads, especially at intersections and under braking conditions.

**Apparatus:** Impact testing machine (13.5–14 kg hammer, 380 mm drop), cylindrical cup (75 mm dia.), sieves (10 mm, 2.36 mm), balance

**Procedure Keywords:** Aggregate (10–12.5 mm) → fill cup in 3 layers (25 tamps each) → 15 blows of hammer → sieve on 2.36 mm → AIV (%) = (fines/total) × 100

**IS Code:** `IS 2386 (Part IV) : 1963` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Typical Values:**

| Application | Maximum AIV |
|---|---|
| Wearing/Surface Course | ≤ 30% |
| Bituminous Macadam | ≤ 35% |
| WBM Base | ≤ 40% |
| Concrete Aggregate | ≤ 30% |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. How many blows are applied in AIV test, and what is the hammer drop height?**
> 15 blows; hammer drop = 380 ± 5 mm; hammer weight = 13.5–14 kg.

**Q2. Why is the cup filled in layers with tamping?**
> To achieve consistent packing density across test samples. Variable packing leads to non-reproducible results.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q3. Why is AIV test more representative of field conditions than ACV?**
> Actual traffic loads are dynamic — vehicles brake, accelerate, and create impact at irregularities. AIV simulates this dynamic nature better than the slow, progressive ACV loading.

**Q4. Can an aggregate pass ACV but fail AIV? What does this indicate?**
> Yes. It indicates a brittle aggregate — high resistance to slow compression but fractures under sudden impact. Such aggregate is unsuitable for pavement wearing course despite acceptable ACV.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q5. For a structural concrete column in a multi-storey building, which is more important — AIV or ACV?**
> ACV, because column loading is predominantly static compressive. AIV is more critical for pavements, bridge decks, and other dynamic-load applications. However, both should be within limits per IS 383:2016 for concrete aggregate.

---

## 2.3 Los Angeles Abrasion Test

**Principle:** Evaluates resistance of aggregate to combined abrasion, impact, and grinding action — simulating wear due to tyre friction in pavement surface courses.

**Apparatus:** LA Abrasion machine (drum 711 mm dia., 508 mm length), steel balls (abrasive charge), sieves (1.70 mm), balance

**Procedure Keywords:** Select grading (A/B/C/D based on size) → load aggregate + steel balls in drum → rotate at 30–33 rpm → 500 revolutions (Grading A/B) or 1000 (C/D) → sieve on 1.70 mm → LA (%) = (loss/original) × 100

**IS Code:** `IS 2386 (Part IV) : 1963` *(Reaffirmed 2021)* | `ASTM C131/C535` (reference)

**Typical Values:**

| Application | Maximum LA Value |
|---|---|
| Concrete (Structural) | ≤ 30% |
| Wearing Course (Pavement) | ≤ 30% |
| Bituminous Macadam | ≤ 35–40% |
| WBM Sub-base | ≤ 50% |

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q1. LA test uses both impact (steel balls) and abrasion (drum rotation). Which effect dominates?**
> Both act simultaneously and cannot be separated. This is actually an advantage — the combined action better replicates the multi-mechanism wear in the field compared to pure abrasion or pure impact tests alone.

**Q2. A pavement has good ACV, AIV, and LA values but develops surface polishing after 5 years. What test was missed?**
> **Polished Stone Value (PSV)** test — measures resistance of aggregate to polishing under repeated tyre action. High PSV → aggregate retains micro-texture → good skid resistance. Low PSV → aggregate polishes smooth → accident hazard especially in wet conditions.

---

## 2.4 Flakiness Index (FI) and Elongation Index (EI)

**Principle:** Shape of aggregate particles influences packing efficiency, voids content, and mechanical performance. Flaky and elongated particles create planes of weakness and increase void content.

**Apparatus:** Metal thickness gauge (slotted), metal length gauge, weighing balance, sieves

**Procedure Keywords (FI):** Sieve aggregate into fractions → pass each fraction through corresponding slot of thickness gauge → weigh particles passing → FI (%) = (weight passing gauge / total) × 100

**Procedure Keywords (EI):** Retain particles on thickness gauge → pass through length gauge → EI (%) = (weight retained on length gauge / total) × 100

**IS Code:** `IS 2386 (Part I) : 1963` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Definition:**
- **Flaky:** Thickness < 0.6 × mean sieve size
- **Elongated:** Length > 1.8 × mean sieve size

**Limits (IS 383:2016 and MoRTH):**

| Parameter | Limit |
|---|---|
| Flakiness Index | ≤ 25% |
| Elongation Index | ≤ 25% |
| Combined FI + EI | ≤ 35% (MoRTH) / ≤ 40% (IS 383) |

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q1. Crushed aggregate from a quarry has FI = 22% and EI = 20%. Combined = 42%. Is it acceptable for M30 concrete?**
> Borderline. IS 383:2016 permits combined FI + EI ≤ 40% for concrete aggregate. At 42%, it exceeds the limit. Reject or blend with more cubical aggregate to bring combined value within 40%.

**Q2. Why do angularly crushed aggregates sometimes have higher FI than naturally rounded gravels?**
> Crushing introduces fracture planes parallel to original rock laminations (in schists, slates, phyllites). These produce thin slab-like flaky particles regardless of the crushing force applied. Rock petrography should be checked before sourcing aggregate.

---

## 2.5 Specific Gravity and Water Absorption of Aggregate

**Principle:** Specific gravity establishes the basis for volumetric mix design calculations. Water absorption indicates porosity of aggregate, affecting the effective w/c ratio and durability of concrete.

**Apparatus:** Wire basket, water tank, balance (accuracy 0.5 g), oven, absorbent cloth, vessel

**Procedure Keywords (Coarse Aggregate):** Saturate sample (24 hr) → surface dry → weigh in air (A) → weigh submerged in water (C) → oven dry → weigh (D)
- Specific Gravity (SSD basis) = A / (A − C)
- Water Absorption (%) = [(A − D) / D] × 100

**IS Code:** `IS 2386 (Part III) : 1963` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Typical Values:**

| Property | Coarse Aggregate | Fine Aggregate |
|---|---|---|
| Specific Gravity | 2.6 – 2.8 | 2.6 – 2.7 |
| Water Absorption | < 2% (preferred) | < 3% |
| Bulk Density (loose) | 1400–1700 kg/m³ | 1500–1800 kg/m³ |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. What is the difference between OD, SSD, and apparent specific gravity?**
> - **OD (Oven Dry):** All pores empty — lowest SG value
> - **SSD (Saturated Surface Dry):** All pores filled with water, surface dry — used in mix design
> - **Apparent SG:** Includes only impermeable volume — highest SG value
> SSD specific gravity is used in concrete mix design (IS 10262).

**Q2. Why is SSD condition most important for mix design?**
> SSD aggregate neither absorbs water from nor contributes water to the mix. It represents the neutral condition for water balance calculation in mix design.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q3. An aggregate has water absorption of 3.5%. What is its site-level implication for concrete mix design?**
> High water absorption (> 2%) means the aggregate will absorb mix water, reducing effective w/c ratio and workability. Site correction: add extra water equal to the absorption (absorption correction). Per IS 10262, aggregate must be pre-wetted to SSD condition or the absorbed water must be accounted for in the design water content.

# 3. WORKABILITY TESTS

---

## 3.1 Slump Test

**Principle:** Measures the consistency of freshly mixed concrete by quantifying the drop (slump) of a concrete cone under its own weight when the mould is removed. Higher slump = higher workability (for a given mix).

**Apparatus:** Slump cone (300 mm height, 200 mm bottom dia., 100 mm top dia.), tamping rod (16 mm dia., 600 mm long, bullet-nosed), steel base plate, ruler

**Procedure Keywords:** Dampen cone and base → fill in 4 layers (each 25 tamps) → level top → lift cone vertically in 5–10 sec → measure vertical drop from top of cone to top of concrete = slump

**IS Code:** `IS 1199 (Part 2) : 2018` *(Revision of IS 1199:1959)*

**Types of Slump:**

| Type | Description | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| True Slump | Uniform drop all around | Normal — acceptable |
| Shear Slump | One side shears off | Indicates harsh/lean mix |
| Collapse Slump | Complete collapse | Very wet mix — unacceptable |

**Recommended Slump Values (IS 456:2000):**

| Concrete Use | Slump (mm) |
|---|---|
| Pavement / Mass concrete | 25 – 50 |
| Lightly reinforced sections | 50 – 75 |
| Beams, columns (RCC) | 75 – 100 |
| Heavily reinforced sections | 100 – 125 |
| Pumped concrete | 100 – 150 |
| Self-compacting concrete (SCC) | Not applicable (flow test) |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. What is slump? How is it measured?**
> Slump = vertical distance the concrete cone subsides after the mould is lifted. Measured from the top of the slump cone (inverted and placed beside) to the top of the slumped concrete.

**Q2. Why is the cone filled in 4 layers with 25 tamps each?**
> Layered filling with rodding ensures uniform compaction throughout the cone height, eliminating air pockets that would give artificially high slump readings.

**Q3. What should be the rate of lifting the slump cone?**
> The cone must be lifted vertically in 5–10 seconds in a steady upward motion — tilting or twisting introduces lateral forces that distort the slump.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q4. Why is slump test not suitable for very dry (zero-slump) or very wet (flowing) concrete?**
> - **Zero slump:** Concrete does not deform — slump gives no information about workability differences between dry mixes.
> - **Very wet (collapse):** Concrete collapses completely — slump equals full cone height (300 mm), losing resolution.
> Vee-Bee test is used for stiff mixes; flow table test for very high workability mixes.

**Q5. What factors increase slump?**
> Increased water content, use of plasticizers/superplasticizers, higher cement content, rounded aggregates, lower fine aggregate content, higher temperature (initially).

**Q6. Can two concrete mixes have the same slump but different workability?**
> Yes. Slump measures consistency, not true workability. A mix with angular aggregates and one with rounded aggregates may show the same slump but behave differently during compaction (internal friction, response to vibration).

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q7. A site foreman adds extra water to increase slump from 50 mm to 100 mm. Explain the consequences.**
> Adding water increases w/c ratio → reduces compressive strength (approximately 5 MPa drop per 0.1 increase in w/c) → increases porosity and permeability → reduces durability → higher risk of segregation and bleeding. IS 456:2000 strictly prohibits addition of water on site without redesigning the mix.

**Q8. What is the slump loss? How is it managed on site?**
> Slump loss = reduction in workability over time after mixing, due to progressive hydration and evaporation. Managed by: (a) using retarders in hot weather, (b) reducing transport time, (c) using ready-mixed concrete with agitator trucks, (d) adding superplasticizer at point of delivery (not extra water).

---

## 3.2 Compaction Factor Test

**Principle:** Determines workability of concrete by measuring the ratio of the weight of concrete compacted by a standard fall (partial compaction) to the weight of the same concrete fully compacted. More workable concrete achieves higher compaction from the same energy input.

**Apparatus:** Two hoppers (upper and lower, conical) and a cylinder (150 mm dia., 300 mm length) mounted on a frame, balance

**Procedure Keywords:** Fill upper hopper → open trapdoor → concrete falls to lower hopper → open trapdoor → concrete falls to cylinder → strike off level → weigh (W₁) → refill cylinder fully compacted → weigh (W₂)
- Compaction Factor (CF) = W₁ / W₂

**IS Code:** `IS 1199 (Part 3) : 2018`

**Typical Values:**

| Workability Level | Compaction Factor | Equivalent Slump |
|---|---|---|
| Very Low (Dry) | 0.70 – 0.75 | 0 – 25 mm |
| Low | 0.75 – 0.85 | 25 – 50 mm |
| Medium | 0.85 – 0.92 | 50 – 100 mm |
| High | 0.92 – 0.95 | 100 – 175 mm |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. Why is CF test more sensitive than slump test for low-workability concrete?**
> At low slump (< 25 mm), slump gives zero or near-zero readings for a wide range of dry mixes — no resolution. CF can differentiate between CF = 0.70 and CF = 0.80 (both showing zero slump), making it more suitable for stiff mixes used in pavements and precast elements.

**Q2. What is the ideal CF value for normal RCC construction?**
> CF of 0.90–0.92, corresponding to medium–high workability (slump ~75–100 mm) for reinforced concrete members with normal reinforcement density.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q3. CF = 1.0 — is this achievable? What does it mean?**
> Theoretically possible but rarely achieved in practice. CF = 1.0 would mean concrete from partial compaction equals fully compacted weight — implying the concrete is self-compacting (SCC). In practice, CF > 0.95 indicates very high workability approaching self-compaction behaviour.

---

## 3.3 Vee-Bee Consistometer Test

**Principle:** Measures the time (Vee-Bee seconds) required for fresh concrete to re-mould and compact under standard vibration, quantifying workability of stiff, zero-slump concrete by its response to vibratory energy.

**Apparatus:** Vee-Bee consistometer (vibrating table, slump cone, transparent disc, electrical timing device)

**Procedure Keywords:** Place concrete by slump cone in pot on vibrating table → remove cone → place transparent disc on surface → switch on vibrator → record time for disc to fully contact concrete (no air under disc) = Vee-Bee time (seconds)

**IS Code:** `IS 1199 (Part 4) : 2018`

**Typical Vee-Bee Values:**

| Workability | Vee-Bee Time (sec) | Slump Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Extremely Dry | > 20 sec | 0 mm |
| Very Stiff | 11 – 20 sec | 0 – 10 mm |
| Stiff | 5 – 10 sec | 10 – 25 mm |
| Stiff-Plastic | 3 – 5 sec | 25 – 50 mm |
| Plastic | < 3 sec | > 50 mm |

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q1. Why is Vee-Bee test preferred for pavement concrete?**
> Pavement concrete is typically designed with very low w/c ratio (0.35–0.45) for durability — resulting in zero or near-zero slump. Vee-Bee seconds differentiate between mixes that would all show zero slump, selecting the optimum workability for mechanical pavers.

**Q2. Which workability test would you recommend for each scenario?**
> - Pavement concrete (stiff mix): **Vee-Bee**
> - Normal RCC (columns, beams): **Slump Test**
> - Mass concrete / lean mixes: **Compaction Factor**
> - Self-compacting concrete: **Flow Table / J-Ring / L-Box**

# 4. STRENGTH OF CONCRETE

---

## 4.1 Compressive Strength Test (Cube Test)

**Principle:** Measures the maximum compressive load a standard concrete cube can sustain before failure. The 28-day cube strength is the characteristic strength used in structural design.

**Apparatus:** Steel cube moulds (150 mm × 150 mm × 150 mm), CTM (capacity ≥ 2000 kN), vibrating table or tamping rod, curing tank at 27°C ± 2°C

**Procedure Keywords:** Cast cubes in 3 layers (tamped / vibrated) → de-mould after 24 hr → cure in water at 27°C → test at 3, 7, 28 days → apply load at 140 kg/cm²/min → record failure load → fc = P/A

**IS Code:** `IS 516 (Part 1) : 2018` *(Revision of IS 516:1959)*

**Characteristic Strength (fck) of Standard Grades (IS 456:2000):**

| Grade | fck (MPa) | Use |
|---|---|---|
| M15 | 15 | PCC, levelling course |
| M20 | 20 | RCC minimum (IS 456) |
| M25 | 25 | Moderate exposure |
| M30 | 30 | Severe exposure / bridges |
| M35 | 35 | Very severe / marine |
| M40 | 40 | Extreme / prestressed |

**Strength Gain Pattern (Approximate):**

| Age | % of 28-day Strength |
|---|---|
| 3 days | 40% |
| 7 days | 65–70% |
| 14 days | 85–90% |
| 28 days | 100% |
| 90 days | 115–120% |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. What is the standard loading rate for compressive strength test?**
> 140 kg/cm²/min (≈ 3.14 kN/s for 150 mm cube). IS 516:2018 specifies this to ensure consistent failure mode.

**Q2. Why is 150 mm cube standard? Can other sizes be used?**
> 150 mm is standard in India. 100 mm cubes can be used for M35 and above (where aggregate size ≤ 20 mm) — but a size correction factor must be applied (100 mm cube gives ~5% higher strength than 150 mm cube due to less lateral restraint effect).

**Q3. Why are cubes cured in water at 27°C ± 2°C?**
> Water at 27°C maintains continuous hydration at a temperature representative of Indian ambient conditions. Drying out between 7–28 days stops hydration → cube strength falls below true concrete potential.

**Q4. What is the rate of loading for 150 mm cube on a CTM with 2000 kN capacity?**
> For M25 concrete: expected failure load ≈ 25 × 150² / 1000 = 562.5 kN. Loading rate = 140 kg/cm²/min = 3.14 kN/sec. Adjust CTM accordingly.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q5. Why is 28 days chosen as the standard age for characteristic strength?**
> The hydration of C₃S and C₂S compounds — primarily responsible for strength — approaches completion by 28 days under standard curing. Strength gain after 28 days is relatively slow, making it a practical and reproducible reference point for design.

**Q6. Why does concrete gain strength beyond 28 days even without curing?**
> C₂S (dicalcium silicate) hydrates slowly over months and years, contributing to long-term strength gain. Atmospheric moisture also continues hydration. However, the rate diminishes — most structural calculations conservatively use 28-day strength only.

**Q7. What is the difference between characteristic strength (fck) and mean target strength (fck + margin)?**
> - **fck:** Strength below which not more than 5% of test results are expected to fall (statistical lower bound).
> - **Target mean strength (ft):** ft = fck + 1.65σ (where σ = standard deviation). Mix must be designed to achieve ft to ensure fck with required reliability (IS 10262:2019).

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q8. Field cube strength (from same batch) is 26 MPa against a design of M30. What action is taken per IS 456?**
> Individual cube results: average of 3 cubes ≥ fck + 3 MPa = 33 MPa; no individual result < fck − 3 MPa = 27 MPa (IS 456, Clause 15). At 26 MPa, the batch fails acceptance criteria. Action: (a) cast additional cubes from the same pour if available; (b) conduct core test on hardened concrete; (c) NDT assessment (rebound hammer + UPV); (d) structural review if cores confirm low strength.

**Q9. Why should concrete cubes NOT be tested immediately after removing from the curing tank?**
> Wet concrete cubes have slightly lower surface strength (free moisture reduces apparent surface hardness). IS 516 requires testing within 30 minutes of removal to avoid significant drying, but surface moisture is blotted before testing. Never test completely dry cubes — they give falsely higher readings.

---

## 4.2 Split Tensile Strength Test

**Principle:** Since concrete cannot be directly tested in tension (due to grip difficulty), indirect tensile strength is determined by applying a compressive line load along the diameter of a cylinder, which generates uniform tensile stress perpendicular to the load.

**Apparatus:** Cylinder moulds (150 mm dia. × 300 mm length), CTM, steel bearing strips (plywood or hardboard, 3 mm thick), jig for alignment

**Procedure Keywords:** Cast and cure cylinder (28 days) → place cylinder horizontally on bearing strips → apply diametric compressive load at 1.2–2.4 kN/s → at failure, cylinder splits along the diameter → fct = 2P / (πLD)

**IS Code:** `IS 5816 : 1999` *(Reaffirmed 2021)*

**Typical Values:**

| Concrete Grade | Approx. Split Tensile Strength |
|---|---|
| M20 | 2.0 – 2.5 MPa |
| M25 | 2.5 – 3.0 MPa |
| M30 | 3.0 – 3.5 MPa |
| Relationship | fct ≈ 0.45 × √fck (IS 456) |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. Write the formula for split tensile strength.**
> fct = 2P / (π × L × D)
> Where: P = failure load (N), L = length of cylinder (mm), D = diameter (mm)

**Q2. Why are bearing strips used in the split tensile test?**
> Bearing strips distribute the line load over a narrow width (to maintain it as a line load) and prevent stress concentration at the contact point, ensuring a clean splitting failure rather than local crushing.

**Q3. What is the typical ratio of split tensile to compressive strength?**
> fct / fck ≈ 8–12% (tensile strength is about 1/10th of compressive strength — concrete is brittle in tension).

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q4. Why is split tensile strength important for designing RCC slabs and pavement?**
> Slabs and pavements develop flexural stresses (tension at bottom). Even though steel carries tension in RCC, the concrete must resist cracking. Split tensile strength directly correlates with cracking resistance — used in mix design for crack-sensitive structures.

**Q5. Two mixes have the same fck but different split tensile strengths. How is this possible?**
> Split tensile strength is influenced by aggregate-paste bond (interface zone), aggregate type, and angularity. Mixes with higher fly ash content, better aggregate-paste bond (low w/c), or more angular aggregates show higher split tensile for the same compressive strength.

---

## 4.3 Flexural Strength (Modulus of Rupture) Test

**Principle:** Measures the tensile strength of concrete in bending — the maximum bending stress at failure. Critical for pavement slabs (IRC:58) where tensile stress at the bottom governs design.

**Apparatus:** Prism moulds (100 × 100 × 500 mm or 150 × 150 × 700 mm), flexure testing machine, two-point loading arrangement

**Procedure Keywords:** Cast prism → cure 28 days → place on roller supports → apply two-point loading at 1/3 span points → record failure load (P) → for fracture within middle third: fr = PL / bd²; for fracture outside: adjusted formula

**IS Code:** `IS 516 (Part 2) : 2018` | `IS 516:1959` (earlier)

**Typical Values:**

| Concrete Grade | Modulus of Rupture (fr) |
|---|---|
| M20 | ≈ 3.1 MPa |
| M30 | ≈ 3.8 MPa |
| M40 | ≈ 4.4 MPa |
| Relationship (IS 456) | fr = 0.7 √fck |

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q1. Why is flexural strength used for pavement design instead of compressive strength?**
> Rigid pavement slabs act as beams — bottom fibres are in tension under wheel loading (Westergaard's analysis). Flexural strength (fr) directly quantifies this tensile capacity. IRC:58:2015 uses fr = 0.7√fck as the design flexural strength input.

**Q2. Why is fr > fct (split tensile strength) for the same concrete grade?**
> In the flexural test, only the extreme fibre reaches the tensile failure stress at any instant — the rest of the cross-section provides restraint (stress gradient effect). In split tensile, a larger volume is at or near maximum tensile stress. This size/gradient effect makes apparent flexural strength higher than true tensile strength.

# 5. NON-DESTRUCTIVE TESTING (NDT)

---

## 5.1 Rebound Hammer Test (Schmidt Hammer)

**Principle:** A spring-released mass impacts the concrete surface; the rebound distance (as % of forward travel) correlates with surface hardness, which in turn correlates with near-surface compressive strength. Higher rebound = harder surface = higher estimated strength.

**Apparatus:** Schmidt rebound hammer (Type N for standard structures; Type L for lightweight; Type NR for charts), calibration anvil

**Procedure Keywords:** Smooth concrete surface (grind if textured) → hold hammer perpendicular (or at defined angle) → press spring release → record rebound index (R) → take minimum 9 readings (3×3 grid, 20–25 mm apart) → discard outliers > ±6 of mean → use correlation chart for estimated strength

**IS Code:** `IS 13311 (Part 2) : 1992` *(Reaffirmed 2022)*

**Rebound Index Interpretation (Approximate — Horizontal Impact):**

| Rebound Number (R) | Estimated Strength | Concrete Quality |
|---|---|---|
| > 40 | > 40 MPa | Excellent |
| 30 – 40 | 25 – 40 MPa | Good |
| 20 – 30 | 15 – 25 MPa | Fair |
| < 20 | < 15 MPa | Poor / Doubtful |
| < 10 | — | Very poor / Carbonated |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. Why are at least 9 readings taken per test area?**
> Concrete is inherently non-homogeneous at the surface. Single readings are unreliable due to local variation in aggregate content, surface finish, and carbonation. Average of 9 (discard ±6 outliers) gives a statistically representative near-surface hardness.

**Q2. How does the hammer orientation affect results?**
> Gravity affects the rebound mass. Horizontal impact gives standard readings. Downward impact → higher rebound (gravity assists return). Upward impact → lower rebound. Correction factors from IS 13311 charts must be applied for non-horizontal testing.

**Q3. What surface preparation is required before testing?**
> Remove loose/laitance layer by grinding. Surface must be smooth, flat, and free from plaster, paint, curing compound, or dust. Wet surfaces give lower rebound.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q4. Why is the rebound hammer test unreliable for concrete older than 6 months?**
> Old concrete undergoes carbonation (CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃) at the surface. The carbonated zone is harder than the inner concrete → gives falsely high rebound → overestimates actual strength. Surface carbonation depth should be checked (phenolphthalein test) if the structure is old.

**Q5. Can rebound hammer replace cube testing for acceptance of concrete?**
> No. IS 13311 Part 2 explicitly states that rebound hammer is NOT a substitute for cube test results. It is an indicative tool for assessment and comparison — not for absolute strength determination or acceptance/rejection of fresh concrete.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q6. Rebound hammer shows R = 38 on a column. UPV shows 3.2 km/s (below 3.5 km/s = doubtful). Which result do you trust for structural assessment?**
> Neither alone — use both together per IS 13311. High R suggests adequate surface hardness; low UPV suggests internal defects or honeycombing. The discrepancy indicates the column may have good surface concrete but poor core quality. Recommend core extraction and testing (IS 516) for confirmation before structural evaluation.

---

## 5.2 Ultrasonic Pulse Velocity (UPV) Test

**Principle:** Ultrasonic pulses (50–54 kHz) travel through concrete; their velocity depends on the elastic modulus and density of concrete — both of which correlate with compressive strength and internal integrity. Voids, cracks, and honeycombs reduce pulse velocity.

**Apparatus:** UPV instrument (PUNDIT or equivalent), transducers (transmitter + receiver, 50–54 kHz), couplant (grease/gel), reference bar for calibration, measuring tape

**Procedure Keywords:** Apply couplant → place transducers (direct/semi-direct/indirect transmission) → measure path length (L) → instrument records transit time (T) → Pulse Velocity (V) = L / T (km/s) → compare with quality chart

**IS Code:** `IS 13311 (Part 1) : 1992` *(Reaffirmed 2022)*

**UPV Quality Classification (IS 13311 Part 1):**

| Pulse Velocity (km/s) | Concrete Quality |
|---|---|
| > 4.5 | Excellent |
| 3.5 – 4.5 | Good |
| 3.0 – 3.5 | Medium (Doubtful) |
| < 3.0 | Poor |

---

### 📋 Basic / Procedure-Based Questions

**Q1. What are the three transmission modes in UPV testing?**
> - **Direct:** Transducers on opposite faces — most accurate (maximum signal strength)
> - **Semi-direct:** Transducers on adjacent faces at 90° — moderate accuracy
> - **Indirect (surface):** Both transducers on the same face — least accurate (used when access to only one face)

**Q2. What is the role of couplant in UPV test?**
> Air gaps between transducer and concrete surface attenuate or block ultrasonic signals. Couplant (petroleum jelly, grease, or acoustic gel) fills micro-gaps, ensuring efficient wave transmission.

**Q3. What frequency transducers are used for concrete testing?**
> 50–54 kHz for standard concrete. Lower frequency (25–40 kHz) for large members or concrete with large aggregates. Higher frequency (100 kHz+) for laboratory specimens.

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q4. Why does UPV decrease in concrete with internal cracks or voids?**
> Ultrasonic waves cannot efficiently propagate through air (in voids/cracks) — they must travel around defects by a longer path. Instrument measures actual path length (straight), not the wave detour → apparent velocity decreases → indicates defect presence.

**Q5. How can UPV and rebound hammer be used together for better structural assessment?**
> Combined assessment (SonReb method): Compressive strength is estimated using both R (rebound) and V (UPV) in combined regression equations. This reduces individual test errors. IS 13311 recommends combined use for field assessment. However, core testing remains the reference for confirmation.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q6. UPV test on a column gives 4.2 km/s (Good) but the column shows surface cracking and spalling. How do you reconcile this?**
> UPV at 4.2 km/s indicates good core concrete quality — the internal mass is sound. Surface cracking and spalling could be due to: (a) carbonation shrinkage, (b) corrosion of reinforcement (cover concrete cracking due to rust volume expansion), (c) thermal cycling stresses. Conduct half-cell potential test (IS 14977) and cover meter survey to assess rebar corrosion — likely cause of the surface distress.

# 6. MIX DESIGN

---

## 6.1 Concrete Mix Design Basics (IS 10262:2019)

**Principle:** Concrete mix design is the process of selecting the optimum proportions of cement, water, fine aggregate, and coarse aggregate to achieve the required strength, workability, and durability at minimum cost.

**IS Code:** `IS 10262 : 2019` — Concrete Mix Proportioning — Guidelines *(4th Revision)*
Supporting codes: `IS 456 : 2000` | `IS 383 : 2016` | `IS 73 : 2013`

---

### 📋 Design Steps (IS 10262:2019 — Keyword Framework)

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Determine target mean strength: ft = fck + 1.65σ |
| 2 | Select w/c ratio from durability and strength criteria |
| 3 | Estimate water content (from Table 2 of IS 10262 based on aggregate size and slump) |
| 4 | Calculate cement content: C = W / (w/c) |
| 5 | Check minimum cement and maximum w/c per IS 456 (Table 5) |
| 6 | Estimate volume of aggregate (coarse and fine) using absolute volume method |
| 7 | Calculate trial mix proportions → cast cubes → test → adjust if needed |

---

### 📋 Key Concepts and Typical Values

**w/c Ratio (IS 456:2000, Table 5):**

| Exposure Condition | Min. Cement (kg/m³) | Max. w/c | Min. fck (MPa) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild | 300 | 0.55 | 20 |
| Moderate | 300 | 0.50 | 25 |
| Severe | 320 | 0.45 | 30 |
| Very Severe | 340 | 0.45 | 35 |
| Extreme | 360 | 0.40 | 40 |

**Standard Deviation (σ) for Target Strength:**

| Degree of Control | σ (MPa) |
|---|---|
| Very Good (batching plant) | 4.0 |
| Good | 5.0 |
| Fair (site mixing) | 6.0 |

---

### 🧠 Conceptual Questions

**Q1. Why is w/c ratio the most important parameter in mix design?**
> Abrams' Law: strength is inversely proportional to w/c ratio (for fully compacted concrete). Beyond water needed for hydration (w/c ≈ 0.23), excess water creates capillary pores on evaporation → reduces strength and durability. Every 0.1 increase in w/c → approximately 4–5 MPa reduction in 28-day strength.

**Q2. What is the difference between nominal mix and design mix?**
> - **Nominal mix (IS 456, Table 9):** Fixed proportions (1:2:4 for M15, 1:1.5:3 for M20) — conservative, less economical. For small/non-critical works only.
> - **Design mix (IS 10262):** Proportions derived from material testing and target strength — accounts for actual material properties → more economical and reliable. Mandatory for M25 and above (IS 456).

**Q3. Why is minimum cement content specified in IS 456?**
> Minimum cement content ensures: (a) adequate paste volume to fill voids between aggregates, (b) sufficient alkalinity to protect reinforcement from corrosion, (c) minimum durability against chemical attack. Reducing cement below the minimum compromises durability even if strength is adequate.

---

### ❗ Tricky Examiner Questions

**Q4. A design mix for M30 uses w/c = 0.45 and cement = 350 kg/m³. The contractor wants to reduce cement to 300 kg/m³ to save cost. What IS code provision applies?**
> IS 456:2000 Table 5 for Severe exposure: minimum cement = 320 kg/m³ and maximum w/c = 0.45. At 300 kg/m³ cement, the mix violates the minimum cement requirement — even if strength is achieved. Durability will be compromised. The contractor cannot reduce cement below the IS 456 minimum regardless of achieved strength.

**Q5. What is the effect of using 53 Grade OPC instead of 43 Grade in the same mix design?**
> Using OPC 53 for the same w/c ratio → higher cement quality → excess strength margin. OR, the mix can be redesigned with lower cement content (maintaining w/c), reducing heat of hydration and cost while achieving the same target strength. IS 10262 recommends redesigning the mix when cement grade changes.

## 7.1 Cement — Storage, Handling, and Field Identification

**Q1. What is the weight of one cement bag? What volume does it occupy?**
> - Weight: 50 kg (standard bag, OPC/PPC)
> - Volume: 50/1440 = **0.0347 m³** (assuming bulk density of cement ≈ 1440 kg/m³)

**Q2. What is the shelf life of cement and how should it be stored on site?**
> - Shelf life: **3 months maximum** from date of manufacture (IS 4031 requires retesting after 3 months)
> - Storage: (a) raised wooden platform (minimum 200 mm above floor), (b) covered with waterproof tarpaulin, (c) minimum 300 mm from walls, (d) FIFO (First In First Out) stock rotation, (e) maximum stack height = 10 bags, (f) airtight shed in monsoon season

**Q3. How many bags of cement per m³ of concrete (approximately)?**

| Mix Grade | Approx. Cement Bags per m³ |
|---|---|
| M20 (Nominal 1:1.5:3) | 8 bags (400 kg) |
| M25 (Design mix) | 8–9 bags |
| M30 (Design mix) | 9–10 bags |

**Q4. How to identify fresh vs. old/deteriorated cement on site (without lab)?**

| Field Test | Fresh Cement | Deteriorated Cement |
|---|---|---|
| Colour | Grey with greenish tint | Off-white, yellowish |
| Lumps | None | Hard lumps (pre-hydration) |
| Feel in hand | Cool (endothermic) | Warm or neutral |
| Float in water | Sinks after floating briefly | Floats for long or sinks immediately |
| IS 4031 Note | Retest if > 3 months old | Reject if lumps cannot be broken |

**Q5. Can lumpy cement be used on site?**
> Only if lumps break easily by hand and the cement passes all IS 4031 tests (especially fineness, setting time, and strength). Hard lumps that cannot be crushed indicate significant pre-hydration → cement is degraded → REJECT.

## 7.2 Concrete Defects — Diagnosis and Prevention

| Defect | Cause | Effect | Prevention |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bleeding** | Excess water, fine aggregate deficiency, high w/c | Water channels at surface → weak surface laitance → reduced durability | Reduce w/c, add fine aggregate, avoid over-vibration |
| **Segregation** | Excess aggregate, high slump, dropping concrete from height | Non-uniform mix, aggregate at bottom, paste on top | Limit drop height ≤ 1.5 m, control slump, proper grading |
| **Honeycombing** | Inadequate compaction, reinforcement congestion, stiff mix | Voids in concrete → structural weakness → corrosion entry | Vibrate properly (immersion vibrator), maintain slump, adequate cover |
| **Plastic Shrinkage Cracking** | Rapid surface evaporation (hot/windy weather) | Surface cracks within hours of casting | Wind breakers, start curing immediately, use evaporation retarder |
| **Crazing** | Excessive trowelling of bleed water, over-finishing | Network of fine surface cracks | Do not finish while bleed water is present |
| **Cold Joints** | Delay in placing successive lifts beyond initial set | Horizontal weakness plane | Limit pour interval to < initial setting time of concrete |
| **Thermal Cracking** | High temperature differential in mass concrete | Through cracks | Pre-cool aggregates, use low-heat cement (PPC/PSC), limit pour size |

## 7.3 Workability Issues on Site

**Q1. Concrete arrives at site with low slump (25 mm) but the specified slump is 75–100 mm. What should the site engineer do?**
> - Check time from batching (slump loss may have occurred)
> - Check temperature and delivery time (monsoon heat in Punjab can cause 20–30 mm slump loss/30 min)
> - **DO NOT add extra water** — this violates IS 456 and reduces strength
> - Correct action: Add approved superplasticizer dose (if within acceptable time), or reject batch and request fresh supply
> - Document non-conformance in site register

**Q2. How is concrete workability affected in winter vs. summer in Punjab?**
> - **Summer (40°C+):** Faster hydration → rapid slump loss → shorter working time → risk of plastic shrinkage. Use retarders, ice in mixing water, early morning pouring.
> - **Winter (< 10°C):** Slower hydration → slower strength gain → risk of frost damage to green concrete. Use warm water for mixing, accelerators if needed; protect freshly cast concrete.

**Q3. What is the maximum time between mixing and placing of concrete (IS 456)?**
> Concrete must be placed within **30 minutes** of mixing (or before initial set begins) when transported by non-agitating equipment. With transit mixers (agitating), the concrete must be discharged within **2 hours** (or 50 revolutions of drum at mixing speed), whichever is earlier.

## 7.4 Curing — Field Practices

**Q1. Why is curing important? What happens if concrete is not cured?**
> Curing maintains moisture for continued hydration. Without curing: (a) surface dries → hydration stops → reduced surface strength, (b) plastic shrinkage cracking, (c) increased permeability → reduced durability, (d) 28-day strength may be 20–40% lower than properly cured concrete.

**Q2. Minimum curing duration as per IS 456:2000?**

| Cement Type | Minimum Curing Period |
|---|---|
| OPC (M20 and below) | 7 days |
| OPC (M25 and above) | 10 days |
| PPC / PSC / Slag cement | 14 days (blended cements hydrate slower) |
| Extreme conditions (hot, dry) | 14 days minimum |

**Q3. Common field curing methods and their effectiveness:**

| Method | Best For | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Ponding | Slabs, flat surfaces | Most effective; impractical for vertical surfaces |
| Jute/Hessian covering | Columns, beams | Must be kept continuously wet |
| Polythene sheeting | Slabs | Prevents evaporation; easy to apply |
| Sprinkler / Water sprinkling | Large areas | Intermittent wetting is insufficient |
| Curing compound | Precast, remote areas | Less effective than wet curing; check compatibility |
| Steam curing | Precast factory | Rapid strength gain; not field use |

**Q4. Can RCC be loaded before 28 days? When is formwork removed?**
> - Formwork removal per IS 456:2000:
>   - Walls, columns, vertical surfaces: **16–24 hours** minimum
>   - Slab soffits (props left under): **3 days**
>   - Beam soffits (props left under): **7 days**
>   - Props to slabs (< 4.5 m span): **7 days**
>   - Props to slabs (> 4.5 m span): **14 days**
>   - Props to beams (> 6 m span): **21 days**

# 8. HIGH-YIELD EXAMINER QUESTIONS

*Commonly asked in PTU external viva — integrative, conceptual, WHY-based.*

---

**Q1. What is the fundamental difference between concrete strength and concrete durability?**
> - **Strength:** Capacity to resist mechanical loads (fck in MPa) — a mechanical property.
> - **Durability:** Ability to maintain required performance over design life under environmental attack (chloride, sulphate, carbonation, freeze-thaw) — a long-term property.
> Both are required for a good structure. A high-strength concrete can be durable with low w/c ratio, but a low-permeability concrete with moderate strength may outlast a high-strength, high-permeability one in aggressive exposure.

**Q2. Why does increasing water content increase workability but decrease strength?**
> Water acts as a lubricant between particles → improves flowability (workability). Excess water beyond hydration requirement (w/c > 0.25–0.30) does not react with cement → on evaporation leaves capillary pores → reduces density, stiffness, and strength. Abrams' Law: fc ∝ 1/(w/c)^n.

**Q3. Why is NDT preferred for assessment of existing structures over destructive testing?**
> - Structure remains intact (no damage)
> - Covers larger area (statistically more representative)
> - Can be repeated over time (monitoring)
> - Faster and cheaper than coring
> However, NDT gives estimated/relative strength — IS 13311 mandates core testing for confirmation when NDT indicates poor quality.

**Q4. Why does lab cube strength consistently exceed site concrete strength?**

| Factor | Lab Cube | Site Concrete |
|---|---|---|
| Compaction | Full (vibrating table) | Variable (manual vibration) |
| Curing | Controlled (27°C, water) | Variable (ambient, often inadequate) |
| w/c ratio | Precisely controlled | May have unauthorized water addition |
| Sampling | Representative (fresh from mixer) | May include delayed pours |
| Expected Difference | 100% reference | 80–90% of cube strength (IS 456 allows 25% lower in-situ) |

**Q5. Why is pumped concrete designed with higher slump than non-pumped concrete?**
> In pumping, concrete must flow under pressure through pipes (diameter 100–150 mm) over horizontal and vertical distances. Friction in pipes and bends requires higher initial workability. However, water should not be added for pumpability — instead, use higher paste content, well-graded aggregates, and superplasticizers.

**Q6. Why is PPC or PSC preferred over OPC for mass concrete (dams, rafts, thick slabs)?**
> OPC generates high heat of hydration (typically 335–400 kJ/kg). In thick sections, this heat cannot escape → interior temperature rises (can exceed 70°C) → large temperature differential with cooler surface → thermal gradient stresses → thermal cracking.
> PPC/PSC generates 20–30% less heat → lower peak temperature → reduced thermal gradient → lower cracking risk. IS 456 recommends low-heat cement for mass concrete.

**Q7. A column test cube fails at 23 MPa against M25 specification. What is the investigation sequence?**
> 1. Check cube casting, curing, and testing records (eliminate testing error)
> 2. Re-examine remaining cubes from the same batch
> 3. Conduct rebound hammer and UPV on the column in situ
> 4. If NDT confirms poor quality → extract cores (IS 516) per IS 456 Clause 17
> 5. Core strength × 1.25 (size factor) → compare with fck
> 6. If cores also low → structural review by competent engineer
> 7. Options: jacketing, grouting, demolition and reconstruction (based on structural analysis)

**Q8. Why are cylinders used in some countries (USA/Europe) and cubes in India for compressive strength?**
> Both are standard specimens. Cylinder (150 mm dia. × 300 mm) gives lower compressive strength than 150 mm cube (approximately 80% of cube strength) due to aspect ratio — slender cylinders have less end-restraint effect. India follows IS 516 (cube); USA follows ASTM C39 (cylinder). Conversion factor: fcylinder ≈ 0.8 × fcube.

**Q9. What is the significance of the water-cement ratio in concrete durability, specifically for chloride environments (coastal Punjab / industrial zones)?**
> Chloride ions penetrate concrete through capillary pores (capillary suction and diffusion). At w/c = 0.55: permeability is high → chlorides reach reinforcement within 10–15 years → depassivation → corrosion. At w/c ≤ 0.40: dense matrix with fewer connected capillaries → chloride diffusion coefficient reduces by 10× → structural life exceeds 50 years. IS 456 mandates w/c ≤ 0.40 for extreme exposure.
